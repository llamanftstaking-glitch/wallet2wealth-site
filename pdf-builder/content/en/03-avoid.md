Crypto has real opportunity. It also attracts every species of scammer on the planet. Before you put a single dollar in, you need to know what they look like — because they're good at what they do.

**The scams that hit beginners hardest**

<img class="content-image" src="{{IMG_03}}" alt="Scam Flow Diagram"/>

Rug pulls are when a team hypes a new coin, collects millions in investment, then disappears overnight. The coin goes to zero. The website goes dark. The Discord mods vanish. This happens constantly with new meme coins and "next big thing" projects. If someone is promising 10x returns on a coin you've never heard of, that's the setup.

Fake giveaways are everywhere on social media. The formula: someone impersonates Elon Musk, Coinbase, or a major exchange and promises to double any crypto you send them. "Send 0.1 ETH, get 0.2 back." Once you send it, it's gone. No legitimate company or person ever runs a "send crypto to get more crypto" promotion. Ever.

:::warning
If someone slides into your DMs offering to help you invest, teach you a "strategy," or claiming they made a fortune and want to share it — block them immediately. These are romance scams and pig butchering scams. They build trust over weeks, then drain your account. Real investors don't cold-message strangers on Instagram.
:::

Seed phrase phishing is the most dangerous attack of all. Your seed phrase (12 or 24 words) is the master key to your wallet. Scammers build fake wallet websites, fake MetaMask support pages, and fake "wallet recovery" tools — all designed to trick you into typing your seed phrase somewhere they can steal it. Once they have it, they drain your wallet in seconds.

:::warning
No legitimate wallet app, exchange, or support team will ever ask for your seed phrase. Not ever. If a website or person asks for it, you are being robbed. Close the tab. Block the account.
:::

**Common beginner mistakes that aren't scams — just expensive**

Buying meme coins first is how most beginners lose money fast. Meme coins spike, get media attention, and then collapse — usually right after beginners buy in. There's nothing wrong with meme coins eventually, but your first purchase should be something that's been around for years and has real liquidity.

Leaving funds on an exchange is a risk most beginners don't think about. When your crypto sits on Coinbase or Binance, you don't actually own it — they do. Exchanges get hacked. They freeze withdrawals. They go bankrupt (FTX took billions of dollars of customer funds down with it in 2022). For anything more than a small amount, move your crypto to a wallet you control.

Sharing your seed phrase, even with someone you trust, is a permanent risk. People get hacked. Phones get stolen. Relationships end badly. The only person who should ever know your seed phrase is you.

Chasing pumps — buying a coin because it's already up 300% — is how people buy the top and watch it crash. The people selling to you when something is pumping are the people who bought it weeks ago.

:::checklist
### Scam Detection Checklist

- Someone promised guaranteed returns or "risk-free" profits
- You were asked to send crypto first to receive more back
- A stranger DM'd you offering investment help or a "hot tip"
- The project has no whitepaper, no named founders, or launched last week
- The website URL looks slightly off (coinbas3.com, metamask-support.net)
- Support asked for your seed phrase or wallet password
:::
