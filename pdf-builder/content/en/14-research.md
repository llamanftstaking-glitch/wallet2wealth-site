Every serious loss in crypto can be traced back to skipping this step.

Someone on Twitter said it was the next 100x. A Discord group was hyped about it. You bought without reading a single page of documentation. Three weeks later, the project was abandoned and the price was down 90%.

"Do Your Own Research" (DYOR) isn't a buzzword. It's the discipline of verifying claims before committing money. Here's exactly how to do it.

## The 5-Question Framework

Before buying any cryptocurrency project, you need honest answers to these five questions:

**1. What does it actually do?**

If you can't explain the project's purpose in two sentences without using the words "revolutionary," "disruption," or "blockchain," you don't understand it. Read the website. Find the whitepaper. If there is no whitepaper, that's your answer.

**2. Who built it?**

Anonymous teams are a red flag — especially for newer projects. Look for verifiable founders with professional histories on LinkedIn. Check if the team has shipped working products before. Pseudonymous developers exist in legitimate projects (like Bitcoin's Satoshi Nakamoto), but the codebase should be open-source and auditable. If you can't verify who built something, don't give them your money.

**3. Is there real usage?**

A blockchain project with no real users is a whitepaper and a token price. Look for on-chain activity — transactions per day, total value locked (TVL) in DeFi protocols, number of active wallets. Tools like Etherscan (Ethereum), Solscan (Solana), or the project's own dashboard show real activity. Empty blockchains are marketing, not technology.

**4. What's the tokenomics?**

"Tokenomics" means how the token supply is structured. Key questions:
- What's the total supply? Is it capped or inflationary?
- How many tokens do the team and early investors hold?
- When do locked tokens get released? (These are called "vesting schedules")
- Is there a purpose for holding the token, or is it purely speculative?

If team and VCs hold 40% of the supply with tokens unlocking next month, you may be buying their exit.

**5. What does the community actually say?**

Not the official Discord — that's marketing. Look for critical discussion on Reddit, independent crypto forums, and niche communities. Search "[Project Name] criticism" and "[Project Name] scam." If every discussion is breathlessly positive with no skeptics, something is wrong. Legitimate projects have critics.

:::detective
**The Whitepaper Test**

Every serious crypto project publishes a whitepaper — a technical document explaining what the project does and how it works. Bitcoin's whitepaper is 9 pages. Ethereum's is detailed and precise.

If a project's whitepaper is vague, uses buzzwords instead of specifications, or doesn't exist at all — walk away. Marketing decks are not whitepapers. A well-written whitepaper shows the team knows exactly what they're building.
:::

## Red Flags That Should Stop You Cold

If you see any of these, stop and re-evaluate:

- **Guaranteed returns.** No legitimate investment guarantees anything. "10% monthly, guaranteed" is always fraud.
- **Anonymous team with no verifiable history.** Fine for Bitcoin in 2009. Not acceptable for a new DeFi protocol in 2025.
- **Pressure to buy now.** "This window closes in 48 hours." Real investment opportunities don't expire. Scams create urgency.
- **Celebrity endorsements.** Celebrities are paid to promote. Their endorsement means nothing about the project's legitimacy.
- **Copied whitepaper.** Yes, this happens. Search key phrases from the whitepaper — some projects paste from other projects and change the name.
- **No real product.** A token without a working product is speculative at best, fraudulent at worst.

## Where to Research

**Primary sources:**
- Project's official website and documentation
- GitHub repository (check commit activity — is anyone actually building?)
- Whitepaper (linked from official site)

**On-chain data:**
- Etherscan.io (Ethereum)
- Solscan.io (Solana)
- CoinGecko and CoinMarketCap (price history, market cap, token distribution)

**Critical perspectives:**
- Reddit (r/CryptoCurrency, r/Bitcoin, project-specific subreddits)
- Twitter/X (search project name + criticism)
- DeFiLlama.com (TVL and protocol activity)

:::action
Pick one project you've been curious about. Before looking at its price, find its whitepaper. Read the first page. Then answer: what does this project actually do, and why does it need a token? If you can't answer both, you're not ready to buy it.
:::
