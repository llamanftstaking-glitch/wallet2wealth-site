Most people who lose money in crypto do the same thing: they try to be clever. They watch charts. They buy when things feel exciting. They sell when things feel terrifying. They repeat this until they're broke.

The strategy that beats them is boring. That's why it works.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging: The Only Strategy You Need Right Now

<img class="content-image" src="{{IMG_16}}" alt="DCA Growth Graph"/>

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) means buying a fixed dollar amount of crypto on a fixed schedule — regardless of price.

$50 every Monday. $100 on the first of the month. Whatever you can afford. You don't check the price first. You don't wait for a dip. You just buy.

Here's what that actually looks like over a year:

$50 per week × 52 weeks = **$2,600 invested**

Some weeks you buy Bitcoin at $60,000. Some weeks you buy it at $40,000. Some weeks at $75,000. Your average cost ends up somewhere in the middle — lower than the highs, higher than the lows. You never buy all-in at the peak. You never miss the bottom waiting for it to drop further.

This is how you remove emotion from the equation. And emotion is what kills most investors.


## The Rule That Protects You First

Before you invest a single dollar in crypto, apply this rule: **only invest money you are genuinely prepared to lose.**

Not "prepared to lose in theory." Prepared to lose in reality — meaning if it went to zero tomorrow, your rent is still paid, your groceries are still covered, and you don't have a panic attack.

Crypto is volatile. Bitcoin has dropped 80% from its peak — multiple times. It has also recovered and made new highs — multiple times. But those recoveries take years. If you invest money you need in six months, you will be forced to sell at the worst time.

Protect your life first. Invest with the rest.

## A Simple Starter Portfolio

You don't need 30 coins. You need this:

- **60% Bitcoin** — the bedrock. Most secure. Most tested.
- **30% Ethereum** — the platform. Real usage. Real ecosystem.
- **10% one researched altcoin** — and only if you've actually read the project's whitepaper.

That last 10% is optional. If you haven't read the whitepaper, skip it. An altcoin you found on Twitter because someone said it would "100x" is not research. A whitepaper is a public document that explains what a project does, how it works, and why it matters. If you can't find it or can't understand it, that's your answer.

## Why Trading Fails Beginners

Studies consistently show that more than 80% of day traders lose money over any significant time period. In crypto — where prices move faster and markets run 24/7 — that number is likely higher.

The problem isn't intelligence. It's information asymmetry. Professional traders have algorithms, real-time data feeds, and years of experience. A beginner with a phone app is not competing on equal footing.

Every trade you make, you pay a fee. Every time you try to time the market, you introduce a decision point where emotion can override logic. The more active you are, the more chances you have to make a mistake.

The data is clear: inactivity outperforms activity for almost every retail investor.

## Think in Years, Not Days

Crypto is a long game. If you buy Bitcoin today and check the price tomorrow, you're going to feel something — probably fear or greed. Both will tempt you to do something stupid.

Set a minimum time horizon of 3 to 5 years. This isn't arbitrary. Most bear markets in crypto have resolved within 2–3 years. Investors who held through the crashes came out ahead. Investors who sold the bottom locked in losses and missed the recovery.

Check your portfolio monthly, not daily. Make decisions quarterly, not hourly.

## When to Sell

Sell when you have a plan — not when you have a feeling.

Write down your exit before you buy. "I will sell 25% if Bitcoin reaches $X." "I will sell if I need the money for Y." That's a plan.

Never sell because the price dropped and you panicked. Never sell because someone on Reddit said the bull run is over. Never sell because you're bored.

The market will test your conviction. Most people fail that test. The ones who don't are the ones who decided in advance what they were going to do.
