Most people jump into crypto without knowing what's running underneath. You don't need a computer science degree. But knowing the basics — what a blockchain is, what gas fees mean, why DeFi carries real risk — will help you make smarter decisions and avoid expensive mistakes.

Here's everything you need. Plain English. No jargon.

## What Is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a public ledger that lives on thousands of computers simultaneously. No company owns it. No single person controls it. Every transaction ever made is recorded there permanently — and because thousands of copies exist at once, no one can quietly alter the history.

Think of it this way: imagine a Google Doc that thousands of people all hold a copy of. Anyone can read it. No single person can secretly edit history. That's blockchain.

This is why crypto is described as "trustless." You don't have to trust a bank or a company. You trust the math and the network.

## What Is a Node?

A node is simply a computer connected to the blockchain network. Nodes verify transactions, store the full transaction history, and keep the network honest.

Nodes are like thousands of independent accountants all checking the same books at once. If one lies, the others reject it.

There are hundreds of thousands of nodes running Bitcoin and Ethereum at any given moment. That's what makes these networks resilient. There's no single server to hack, no single point of failure.

## What Are Gas Fees?

Every transaction on a blockchain requires a small fee paid to the network — called a gas fee. This fee compensates the nodes and validators who process your transaction.

Gas fees aren't fixed. They go up when the network is congested — like a highway during rush hour — and drop when traffic is light. On Ethereum especially, gas fees can spike dramatically during peak periods, making small transactions temporarily expensive. Timing your transactions for off-peak hours can save you real money.

Bitcoin has its own version of fees, which also fluctuate with network demand.

## What Are Smart Contracts?

A smart contract is self-executing code that lives on a blockchain. It runs automatically when certain conditions are met — no human, no middleman, no bank required.

A smart contract is like a vending machine — put in the right inputs, it automatically gives you the output. No human needed.

Smart contracts power almost everything interesting in crypto: decentralized exchanges, lending protocols, NFT marketplaces, and entire financial systems. If a condition in the code is met, the contract executes. Always. Automatically.

## What Is DeFi?

DeFi stands for Decentralized Finance. It's a collection of financial services — lending, borrowing, trading, earning yield — that run entirely on smart contracts instead of traditional banks or brokers.

With DeFi, you can lend out your crypto and earn interest, borrow against your holdings without a credit check, or swap tokens instantly without an exchange account. The entire system runs 24/7 with no closing hours and no gatekeepers.

<img class="content-image" src="{{IMG_23}}" alt="DeFi Risk Visual"/>

:::warning
DeFi is powerful but complex and risky — beginners should learn the basics first before touching it. Smart contract bugs, protocol exploits, and liquidation events have wiped out millions. Do not deposit money into DeFi protocols until you fully understand how they work.
:::

## What Are Stablecoins?

Stablecoins are cryptocurrencies designed to hold a fixed value — usually $1 USD. The most widely used are USDC and USDT.

They're not investments. They're tools. Traders use them to park value between trades without converting back to dollars. People use them for fast, borderless transfers. Think of them as digital cash that lives on the blockchain — useful, but not something you hold expecting it to grow.

## What Is Staking?

Staking means locking up your crypto to help secure a blockchain network. In return, you earn rewards — new tokens issued by the network as payment for your participation.

It's often compared to earning interest in a savings account, but the analogy only goes so far. Staking rewards are paid in crypto, so if the price drops, your rewards drop in dollar value too. Some platforms also lock your funds for a set period, meaning you can't sell even if the market moves against you. Understand both the upside and the risk before you stake anything.

## Market Cap

Market cap is calculated by multiplying the current price of a coin by its total circulating supply — it represents the total value of that cryptocurrency. A higher market cap generally means a more established, more liquid asset. A lower market cap means higher potential returns — but also higher volatility and higher risk.

When you see a coin with a $500M market cap next to one with a $500B market cap, you're looking at very different risk profiles, even if the price per coin is similar.
