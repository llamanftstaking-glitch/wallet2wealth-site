In November 2022, FTX — one of the biggest crypto exchanges on the planet — collapsed in 72 hours. Eight billion dollars in customer funds: gone. Accounts frozen overnight. No warning. People woke up and their savings simply didn't exist anymore.

That wasn't a hack. That was a company failing. And if your crypto lives on an exchange, the same thing can happen to you.

## Exchanges Are Not Vaults

When you hold crypto on Coinbase or Binance, you don't own crypto. You own a promise. The exchange holds the actual coins and gives you a number on a screen. If they get hacked, go bankrupt, or freeze withdrawals — your number goes to zero.

Real ownership means holding crypto in your own wallet. When you control the private key, no company can freeze it, lose it, or gamble with it.

This is what "not your keys, not your coins" actually means. It's a warning, not a slogan.

## Your Seed Phrase Is the Master Key

We said this in the last section. We're saying it again because people keep losing money over it.

Your 12-word seed phrase is the only thing standing between you and losing everything. Anyone who has those 12 words owns your wallet. Period. No exceptions.

- Write it on paper. Two copies. Store them in different places.
- Never type it into a website, app, or chat.
- Never photograph it or save it in your notes app.
- Never share it with "support" — no legitimate support team will ever ask.

There is no recovery. No customer service line. No "forgot my seed phrase" button. If you lose it, your crypto is gone forever.

## Turn On 2FA — But Do It Right

Two-factor authentication adds a second lock to your account. Always enable it. But SMS (text message) 2FA is weak.

SIM swapping is a real attack. A hacker calls your phone carrier, pretends to be you, and gets your number transferred to their phone. Now they receive your SMS codes. It takes one phone call and costs them nothing.

Use an authenticator app instead — Google Authenticator or Authy. The code lives on your device, not your phone number. It cannot be SIM-swapped.

Set this up on every crypto account you create.

## Phishing: The #1 Way People Get Robbed

Phishing is simple. Someone pretends to be a trusted company — MetaMask, Coinbase, Ledger — to trick you into handing over your seed phrase or logging into a fake site.

It looks like this:

- An email that says "Your account has been compromised. Verify now." The link goes to a fake Coinbase site that steals your password.
- A Google ad for "MetaMask download" that installs malware instead.
- A DM on Twitter or Discord from "MetaMask Support" asking for your seed phrase to "fix" an issue.

Real story: In 2021, a Ledger hardware wallet user received a fake "security alert" email after Ledger's customer database was leaked. The email looked exactly like an official Ledger message. He clicked the link, entered his seed phrase on a fake site, and lost $30,000 in under a minute.

The support team didn't ask for his seed phrase. The fake site did.

The rule is simple: No one legitimate will ever ask for your seed phrase. Ever. If someone asks, it's a scam. End the conversation.

:::warning
The #1 way people get hacked is clicking a link in an email or DM and entering their seed phrase or password on a fake website. Bookmark the real sites you use. Never click links to crypto sites from email, search ads, or social media messages. Type the URL directly.
:::

:::checklist
### Security Checklist

- Moved crypto off the exchange into your own wallet
- Seed phrase written on paper and stored in two separate physical locations
- Seed phrase never photographed, never typed into any app or website
- 2FA enabled on every crypto account using an authenticator app (not SMS)
- Bookmarked the real URLs for every platform you use — never click links from email
- "MetaMask Support" or any crypto support DM asking for your seed phrase? Block and report immediately
- Downloaded wallet software only from official sites (MetaMask.io, not Google ads)
:::
