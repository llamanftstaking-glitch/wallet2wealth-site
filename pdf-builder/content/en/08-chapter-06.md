Every few months, someone discovers crypto for the first time during a bull market. The charts are green, Twitter is euphoric, their friend just made 400% on some token they'd never heard of, and everyone seems to be getting rich. They buy. The market peaks. The crash arrives. They panic-sell at a loss and conclude that crypto is a scam.

This is not bad luck. It is a failure to understand cycles.

Markets are not random. They move through identifiable patterns driven by human psychology, liquidity, network growth, and structural events. The investor who understands these patterns doesn't eliminate risk — nothing does — but they make decisions from clarity rather than emotion. That's the edge. This chapter builds it.

## 6.1 Market Cycles — The Rhythm of Crypto

Every asset market moves in cycles. Crypto's are more extreme than most, but the underlying psychology is the same as stocks, commodities, and real estate: greed drives prices above fundamentals, fear drives them below, and the long-run trend of a growing network points upward.

A **bull market** is a sustained period of rising prices, expanding public interest, media coverage, and new entrants buying in. It feels like it can go on forever. It doesn't.

A **bear market** is the correction — often brutal in crypto, where 70–85% drawdowns from the peak are historical norms, not anomalies. During a bear market, projects fail, speculators leave, and the news is relentlessly negative. This is also when serious investors accumulate quietly.

The crucial insight: **cycle position matters more than asset selection for most investors.** Buying an average asset at the start of a bull market outperforms buying an excellent asset at the top. Timing the exact peak or trough is impossible. Understanding roughly where you are in the cycle is not.

Signals that suggest a market top is forming: mainstream media coverage turns breathless, taxi drivers and coworkers are asking which coin to buy, leverage across exchanges is at all-time highs, and new projects with no fundamentals are seeing massive returns.

Signals that suggest a market bottom is near: capitulation selling (sharp drops on high volume), long-term holders stop selling, media interest falls off entirely, and crypto companies are laying off staff.

## 6.2 Bitcoin Dominance and Altcoin Season

**Bitcoin dominance** is the percentage of total cryptocurrency market capitalization that belongs to Bitcoin. When dominance is high, it means capital is concentrated in Bitcoin — typically a sign that investors are in risk-off mode, preferring the relative safety of the leading asset.

When Bitcoin dominance falls, capital is rotating into altcoins — smaller, higher-risk tokens with more volatile price action. This rotation is what traders call **altcoin season**: a period when smaller assets dramatically outperform Bitcoin, sometimes by factors of 5x, 10x, or more in compressed timeframes.

The typical pattern in a bull cycle: Bitcoin leads the rally first, achieving new highs and pulling institutional and retail attention. Then Ethereum picks up momentum. Then altcoins — first the established ones (Solana, Avalanche), then progressively smaller and more speculative tokens. The euphoria cascades down the risk curve.

Understanding this sequencing helps you contextualize what you're seeing. If small-cap altcoins are mooning while Bitcoin is flat, you're likely late in a cycle rotation, not at the beginning of a new bull market.

## 6.3 On-Chain Metrics — What the Blockchain Actually Tells You

Unlike stocks, crypto markets generate real-time, publicly verifiable data about network activity. On-chain metrics are readings taken directly from the blockchain — not analyst opinion, not company guidance, not media narrative. Just raw network behavior.

Three to understand first:

**Active Addresses** — The number of unique addresses sending or receiving on a given day. Rising active addresses mean the network is being used by more people. Falling active addresses during a price rally is a warning sign — price is moving without the underlying network growing. That gap tends to close by price falling, not addresses rising.

**Exchange Flows** — When large amounts of Bitcoin move from wallets into exchanges, it typically signals intent to sell (you need to move to an exchange to sell). When Bitcoin flows out of exchanges into private wallets, it signals long-term holding intent. High exchange inflows during a price rally suggest distribution — large holders selling to retail buyers. Watch for this.

**MVRV Ratio (Market Value to Realized Value)** — This one requires a bit of setup, but it's worth it. "Realized Value" is calculated by valuing every Bitcoin at the price it last moved, rather than the current price. Think of it as the average cost basis of all holders. The MVRV ratio is Market Cap divided by Realized Cap. When MVRV is very high (say, above 3.5), it means the average holder is sitting on large unrealized gains — historically a signal of market overheating. When MVRV is below 1, the market is trading below average cost basis — historically a signal of deep undervaluation and a potential accumulation zone. It's not a perfect timer, but it's a compass.

"In crypto, the blockchain doesn't lie. The price might mislead you, the headlines will mislead you, and the influencers will definitely mislead you. The chain shows you what people are actually doing with their money."
:::

## 6.4 Technical Analysis — The Useful Minimum

Technical analysis (TA) has devotees who see patterns in everything and skeptics who dismiss it entirely. The truth is somewhere reasonable: a few core concepts are genuinely useful for understanding price context. The elaborate systems — dozens of indicators, complex Elliott Wave counts, 12-timeframe setups — produce diminishing returns and infinite rabbit holes.

**Support and Resistance** are the most useful concepts. Support is a price level where buying has historically overcome selling — a floor. Resistance is where selling has historically overcome buying — a ceiling. These levels matter because they represent where large amounts of capital previously changed hands, creating psychological anchors for future decision-making. A resistance level that gets broken (price pushes through it) often becomes support in subsequent pullbacks.

**Trend Lines** provide context. A series of higher lows on a chart defines an uptrend. Lower highs define a downtrend. Trading against the trend requires a stronger reason than trading with it.

**Volume** is the truth detector for price moves. A breakout above resistance on high volume is more meaningful than one on thin volume. A sharp price drop on low volume is less alarming than the same drop on massive volume. Price tells you what happened; volume tells you how seriously to take it.

That's enough TA to be useful. Don't buy a course. Spend time looking at charts on TradingView and let the patterns become intuitive — they will, faster than you expect.

## 6.5 The Bitcoin Halving Cycle

Every 210,000 blocks — roughly every four years — the Bitcoin network undergoes a **halving**: the reward paid to miners for producing each new block is cut in half. When Bitcoin launched, miners earned 50 BTC per block. After the first halving in 2012, it became 25. Then 12.5. Then 6.25. After the 2024 halving, it's 3.125. This continues until around 2140, when the last Bitcoin will be mined.

Why does this matter for price? Supply shock. Miners are among the most consistent sellers in the market — they sell Bitcoin to cover operational costs. A halving cuts the daily supply of new Bitcoin entering the market by 50% overnight. If demand stays constant while supply is cut, price must eventually rise.

Historically, the 12–18 months following each halving have produced Bitcoin's most significant bull runs. This is not guaranteed to repeat. But it is structural, predictable, and grounded in basic supply and demand logic — not speculation or wishful thinking.

The halving also functions as a market coordination signal. Institutional investors, analysts, and sophisticated traders have it circled on their calendars years in advance. Anticipation of the event influences behavior before it arrives. By the time a halving actually happens, some of its impact has already been priced in — but historically not all of it.

:::detective
**The 2012 Halving — Nobody Was Watching**

When Bitcoin's first halving occurred in November 2012, the event was barely noticed outside a small community of cryptography enthusiasts. The reward dropped from 50 BTC to 25 BTC. Bitcoin's price at that moment: roughly $12.

In the 12 months that followed, Bitcoin reached $1,000. A gain of over 8,000%.

No institutional money was involved. No ETFs. No public awareness. Just a network growing organically, a supply shock nobody on Wall Street was watching, and early believers who understood the mechanism. The 2016 and 2020 halvings produced similar patterns — each with more participants, more capital, and still significant returns. The edge shrinks as more people understand it. But the mechanism doesn't change.
:::

## 6.6 Fear & Greed and the Psychology of the Crowd

The **Crypto Fear & Greed Index** is a composite score from 0 (extreme fear) to 100 (extreme greed) that aggregates volatility, trading volume, social media sentiment, surveys, and market momentum into a single daily reading. It's a blunt instrument. It's also useful precisely because of that simplicity.

Contrarian investors treat it as a guide: when the index is in extreme fear (sub-20), it often signals overselling and potential opportunity. When it's in extreme greed (above 80), it signals euphoria and elevated risk. The index doesn't tell you when to buy or sell to the day. It tells you what emotional state the market is in — and emotional states at extremes tend to revert.

The index is available at alternative.me and is updated daily. It takes two seconds to check. Incorporating a quick glance at it before making any significant move builds a useful discipline of asking: "Am I about to make a fear-based decision or a greed-based one?"

## 6.7 Crypto Twitter and Not Getting Manipulated

Crypto Twitter (now X) is simultaneously the best real-time information source for crypto and one of the most sophisticated manipulation environments ever constructed. Both things are true.

The useful part: breaking news travels faster on Crypto Twitter than anywhere else. Protocol updates, exchange hacks, regulatory announcements, and market-moving events often surface there first. Following a curated list of developers, on-chain analysts, and researchers provides genuine signal.

The dangerous part: financial incentives are almost never disclosed. An influencer with 500,000 followers might hold a token and tweet about it without mentioning that their bags are full. Coordinated pump-and-dump groups use Twitter as a broadcasting tool. Projects pay for positive coverage without labeling it.

Practical rules: accounts that post price predictions constantly are less useful than those who explain mechanisms. Look for people who were publicly right about things that didn't benefit them. Be deeply skeptical of anyone urgently promoting a specific token. Notice who discusses market structure and on-chain data versus who mostly talks about price.

The best signal: what are the builders doing? GitHub activity, protocol updates, and developer hiring tell you more about a project's trajectory than any influencer's thread.
